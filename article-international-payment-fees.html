International Payment Fees:
What PayPal and Stripe Really Charge

📅 March 14, 2026 ⏱️ 6 min read ✏️ Feexio Team
Advertisement · 728×90

Freelancers and online sellers who work with international clients quickly discover that cross-border payments carry a significant hidden cost. Between international transaction surcharges and currency conversion spreads, you can lose 4–7% of a payment before it lands in your local bank account. Here's exactly what each platform charges — and how to minimize the damage.

The Two Layers of International Payment Fees

International payments typically incur two separate costs:

  • Cross-border/international transaction fee: A surcharge added because the payment crosses national borders.
  • Currency conversion fee: Applied when the payment currency differs from your account currency. The processor converts at a rate that includes a spread — meaning you get slightly less than the market exchange rate.

Both fees can apply simultaneously, and both are often buried in the fine print.

PayPal International Fees

PayPal's international fee structure has multiple layers:

  • Standard processing: 2.9% + fixed fee (same as domestic)
  • International transaction fee: Additional 1.5% on top of standard rate
  • Currency conversion: 3–4% spread over the base exchange rate
  • Fixed fee by currency: The $0.30 fixed component changes depending on the transaction currency

Example: A US freelancer receiving $500 from a UK client in GBP would pay 2.9% + 1.5% + currency conversion spread. Total cost: potentially $22–$30 on a $500 payment.

Stripe International Fees

Stripe is more transparent about international fees:

  • Base rate: 2.9% + $0.30 (US domestic)
  • International card fee: +1.5% for cards issued outside the US
  • Currency conversion: +1% when currency conversion is required

Total for an international transaction with currency conversion: approximately 5.4% + $0.30. This is more predictable than PayPal and significantly lower on the conversion spread.

Wise (Formerly TransferWise): The Alternative

For large international payments, Wise has become the default choice for many freelancers and sellers. Wise charges a small flat fee (typically 0.4–1%) and converts at the mid-market exchange rate — meaning you get the real rate without a spread.

On a $2,000 international transfer, the difference between PayPal's conversion spread and Wise's mid-market rate can be $50–$100 in your favor. For freelancers billing international clients regularly, this adds up to thousands annually.

Practical Strategies for Reducing International Fees

  • Invoice in your client's currency: If your client pays in their local currency and you convert to yours, you have more control over the conversion tool you use.
  • Use Wise for receiving international payments: Wise gives you local bank details in major currencies (USD, EUR, GBP, AUD), so clients can pay locally and you receive in your currency at mid-market rates.
  • Build fees into your rate: If you regularly receive international payments, factor the 4–5% loss into your quoted price so it doesn't erode your margin.
  • Consolidate payments: Fewer, larger transfers mean fewer fixed fees relative to the total amount.

Which Processor Is Best for International Work?

There's no single answer — it depends on your volume, currencies, and client preferences. For occasional payments under $200, PayPal is fine. For regular large invoices to international clients, Stripe or Wise will almost always save you money. For very large transfers, a dedicated international payments service like Wise, Payoneer, or even a bank wire may be most cost-effective.
