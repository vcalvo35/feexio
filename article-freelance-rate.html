How to Set Your Freelance
Hourly Rate in 2025

📅 March 5, 2025 ⏱️ 7 min read ✍️ Feexio Team
Advertisement · 728×90

Undercharging is the most common — and most damaging — mistake freelancers make. It's not just about leaving money on the table. Charging too little creates unsustainable workloads, attracts difficult clients, and makes it impossible to invest in your own growth. Getting your rate right from the start is the most important financial decision you'll make as an independent worker.

Why "What Do Others Charge?" Is the Wrong Question

Most freelancers start by looking at what competitors charge. That's understandable, but it leads to race-to-the-bottom pricing. The correct starting point is your own economics: what do you need to earn to live and thrive?

Market rates set a ceiling. Your costs set the floor. Your rate needs to live above the floor.

The Formula: Your Minimum Viable Rate

The calculation is simple but powerful:

  • Required Income = Desired Monthly Income + Monthly Business Expenses
  • Minimum Hourly Rate = Required Income ÷ Billable Hours Per Month

Let's walk through a real example.

Step 1: Define Your Desired Monthly Income

This is what you want to take home after paying yourself — your personal salary equivalent. Be honest and specific. Think about rent/mortgage, groceries, transport, savings contributions, and discretionary spending. Let's say: $4,500/month.

Step 2: Add Your Business Expenses

As a freelancer, you have costs a salaried employee doesn't:

  • Software subscriptions (design tools, project management, accounting)
  • Health insurance (if not covered by a partner's plan)
  • Self-employment taxes (~15.3% in the US on net earnings)
  • Internet, phone, office supplies
  • Marketing, website hosting, courses
  • Retirement savings (SEP-IRA, Solo 401k)

A realistic monthly expense total: $1,500/month.

Required Income = $4,500 + $1,500 = $6,000/month

Step 3: Calculate Realistic Billable Hours

A huge mistake: assuming you'll bill 40 hours × 4 weeks = 160 hours/month. You won't. As a freelancer, a significant portion of your time goes to:

  • Client communication and admin
  • Business development and proposals
  • Invoicing, bookkeeping
  • Learning and skill development
  • Downtime between projects

A realistic billable hours target for an experienced freelancer: 100–120 hours/month. Let's use 100.

Step 4: The Math

  • Minimum Rate = $6,000 ÷ 100 hours = $60/hour

This is your floor — not your rate. If you want a buffer for slow months or growth investment, add 20–30%:

  • Target Rate = $60 × 1.25 = $75/hour

Project-Based Pricing: The Better Option

Once you know your hourly floor, you can convert to project rates — which most experienced freelancers prefer because:

  • Clients focus on value, not hours
  • You benefit from efficiency as you get faster
  • It removes the awkward "hours debate"

To price a project, estimate the hours honestly, then multiply by your hourly rate (or higher). Add a 15–20% buffer for scope creep.

How to Justify a Higher Rate

Raising rates feels scary. But clients don't hire on price alone — they hire on confidence, portfolio, and communication. Here's what actually moves the needle:

  • Specialize: Generalists compete on price. Specialists command premiums. A "web designer" earns less than a "conversion-focused landing page designer for SaaS companies."
  • Show outcomes, not deliverables: Don't say "I'll build a website." Say "I'll build a site that converts visitors into leads."
  • Social proof: Testimonials and case studies make price objections disappear.
  • Raise rates with new clients first: Test higher rates on new prospects before raising them with existing clients.

Factor in Payment Processing Fees

If you accept payment via PayPal or Stripe, don't forget that 2.9% + $0.30 comes off every invoice. On a $1,000 invoice, you lose $29.30. Over a year, at $8,000/month, that's over $2,800 in fees. Include this in your expense calculations — or pass it to clients explicitly.

The Bottom Line

Your freelance rate should never be a guess. It's a math problem with a specific answer based on your real costs. Calculate your floor, add a buffer, test it in the market, and raise it every 6–12 months. Clients who value your work will stay. The ones who don't were never the right clients anyway.
