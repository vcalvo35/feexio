Cost Per Unit: How to Calculate It
and Why It Matters for Sellers

📅 March 9, 2026 ⏱️ 5 min read ✏️ Feexio Team
Advertisement · 728×90

If you sell physical products — whether handmade goods, manufactured items, or bulk materials — knowing your cost per unit is non-negotiable. It's the foundation of every pricing decision you make, and getting it wrong is one of the most common reasons small product businesses fail to make money even when sales are strong.

What Is Cost Per Unit?

Cost per unit is the total cost to produce, acquire, or deliver a single unit of your product. It includes not just raw materials, but every cost that can be attributed to producing that unit.

The basic formula is:

  • Cost Per Unit = Total Production Costs ÷ Number of Units Produced

What to Include in Your Unit Cost

Most sellers underestimate their unit cost because they only count direct materials. A complete unit cost calculation includes:

  • Direct materials: Raw materials, components, ingredients used in the product
  • Direct labor: Time spent producing the item (your time has a cost too)
  • Packaging: Boxes, bags, labels, tissue paper, tape, inserts
  • Shipping supplies: Mailers, padding, void fill
  • Payment processing fees: The 2.9% + $0.30 you pay Stripe or PayPal per sale
  • Platform fees: Etsy listing fees, Amazon FBA fees, Fiverr's 20% if applicable
  • A portion of fixed overhead: Equipment depreciation, workspace costs allocated per unit

Many sellers forget payment processing fees and platform fees entirely — yet these can add 3–25% to the real cost of each sale.

A Practical Example

Let's say you make soy candles and produce 50 per batch:

  • Soy wax (batch): $18.00 → $0.36/unit
  • Fragrance oils (batch): $12.00 → $0.24/unit
  • Wicks (50 units): $6.00 → $0.12/unit
  • Jars (50 units): $45.00 → $0.90/unit
  • Labels (50 units): $8.00 → $0.16/unit
  • Packaging/mailer (per unit): $0.85
  • Labor (3 hours at $20/hr ÷ 50 units): $1.20/unit
  • Payment processing (on $25 sale): ~$1.03/unit
  • Total cost per unit: ~$4.86

At a $25 selling price, your actual margin is ($25 − $4.86) ÷ $25 = 80.6% — but only if you calculated all costs correctly. Miss the labor or packaging and your true margin is much lower than you think.

How Volume Affects Unit Cost

One of the most powerful levers in product businesses is scale. Fixed costs spread across more units reduce the cost per unit significantly:

  • Producing 50 candles → fixed cost per unit: $1.20 (labor allocation)
  • Producing 200 candles → same equipment, less labor per unit if batch process improves: cost per unit drops
  • Buying materials in bulk typically reduces direct materials cost 15–30%

Understanding this relationship helps you make informed decisions about minimum order quantities, pricing tiers, and when it makes sense to increase production volume.

Using Cost Per Unit for Pricing

Once you know your true unit cost, pricing becomes mathematical. Decide on your target margin and back into the price:

  • Target price = Unit Cost ÷ (1 − Target Margin %)
  • Example: $4.86 cost, 75% target margin → $4.86 ÷ 0.25 = $19.44 minimum price
